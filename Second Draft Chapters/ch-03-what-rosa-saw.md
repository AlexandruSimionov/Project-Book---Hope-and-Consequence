# Chapter 3: What Rosa Saw

**March 14, 1985 — and after**

---

The boy was drowning.

Rosa did not know how she had come to be here — watching, bodiless, suspended in a light that had no source and cast no shadow. She had no hands. She had no throat. She was a point of attention, nothing more, and the boy was in water, and the water was pulling him under.

He was small. Eight, maybe nine. Dark-haired, thin-armed, his mouth opening and closing like a fish lifted out of a bucket. The water around him churned with his panic — white froth, the chop of small limbs beating a surface that would not hold. His eyes were wide and animal, and Rosa could see the exact moment when the terror shifted from *I might go under* to *I am going under*, and she tried to scream but had no lungs, tried to reach but had no arms, tried to move toward him but did not know how to move toward anything when she was nothing, when she was only seeing, only this unbearable act of witness.

Then another boy appeared. Older, twelve or thirteen, darker-skinned, moving through the water with the clumsy purpose of someone who had not thought about what he was doing, only that he had to do it. He grabbed the drowning boy beneath the arms and hauled upward. For a moment both boys sank — the weight of two bodies, the drag of wet clothing — and then they surfaced together, gasping, the older boy kicking hard toward the pool's edge, and the drowning boy coughed and water came out of him in a long, thin rope, and he breathed.

Rosa did not know these children. She knew them the way you know a word on the tip of your tongue. The shape of it, the weight. Not the sound.

The light moved.

She was somewhere else. A room without windows, lit by a cold blue glow from a machine she did not understand. The dark-haired boy was older here — mid-twenties, bent over a workbench, his fingers stained with something. He was holding a membrane up to the light: thin as onion skin, iridescent, catching the blue glow and breaking it into colors. He turned it in his hands the way Rosa used to turn her reading glasses, checking for smudges. The concentration in his face was so intense it looked like prayer.

She wanted to speak to him. She wanted to say: *Eat something. You're too thin. Sit up straight.* The vocabulary of care that had been her native language for thirty-eight years in a second-grade classroom. But she had no mouth, and he could not hear her, and she was not there.

The light moved again.

She was above a city — not flying, not looking down, but seeing it the way you see a map, all at once, the whole pattern. Streets and buildings and parks and the bright threads of rivers, and connecting all of it a web of light she had no name for: not electricity, not roads, but something finer, something alive. Each thread ran between two points, and each point was a person, and each person was a knot from which more threads extended, and the web was vast and intricate and trembling with motion, the whole thing breathing like a single organism, and Rosa understood without understanding that she was seeing something that was not meant for human eyes. That what she was being shown was a kindness, or an accident, or both.

Then she saw the angel.

No — she saw something, and her mind reached for the word *angel* the way it would reach for the word *mother* upon seeing her mother's face. The word arrived without effort, without decision, the only word that fit.

It was not a figure with wings. It was not a figure at all. It was a presence — a density of attention, a place where the light gathered and thickened and became heavy with something Rosa could only describe as care. Like being seen completely for the first time. Like standing in a room you thought was empty and realizing someone had been sitting in the corner all along, watching, patient, aching with the effort of not intervening.

The presence had no face. But it had an expression. Rosa felt it the way she felt weather — not with her eyes but with the surface of her whole self, whatever her self was now. The expression was one she recognized from decades of teaching: the look of someone who sees a child about to make a terrible mistake and cannot say anything. Can only watch. Can only hold their breath and hope.

*Who are you waiting for?* Rosa tried to ask.

The presence did not answer. But the light shifted, and Rosa saw more: the dark-haired boy, older still, standing on a balcony at night, gripping the railing with both hands. A woman in a yellow headscarf, laughing. A classroom full of small wooden desks and a teacher whose face was in shadow. An old woman leaning over a hospital bed — herself? No. Someone else, someone decades from now. And connecting all these images, the same threads she had seen in the web above the city, the same trembling filaments of light, running between faces she did not know and could not follow.

She saw the angel again. The presence was saturated with something old and structural — grief held so long it had become load-bearing, like the way a wall can crack and still hold up a ceiling. Rosa thought: *You poor thing. You poor, exhausted thing. How long have you been carrying this?*

The light began to fade.

Rosa reached for it with whatever she was — this bodiless point of seeing, this stripped-down attention — and the vision receded like a wave pulling back from shore, leaving behind not darkness but the ordinary world: cold, noise, a dull red pain that started in her chest and radiated outward through the rest of her like ink dropped into water.

---

She opened her eyes to fluorescent light and a ceiling made of perforated tiles.

The pain was enormous — a boulder on her chest, a structural thing, the kind of pain that told her body it had been broken and put back together by someone in a hurry. She tried to breathe and the air scraped through her throat like sandpaper. She had been intubated. She would learn this later.

Someone was holding her hand. The hand was warm and slightly damp.

"Mrs. Chen? Can you hear me?"

A young woman's face, close. Bloodshot eyes, a loose strand of hair stuck to her temple with sweat, and beneath the exhaustion an expression Rosa recognized — the face of a person who had just done something impossible and had not yet decided whether to believe it.

"There were lights," Rosa said. Her voice sounded wrong. Scraped out. Like the last inch of honey from a jar.

"You're in the hospital, Mrs. Chen. You had a cardiac event. Your heart stopped for eighteen minutes."

*Eighteen minutes.* The number meant nothing to her and everything. She had been gone for eighteen minutes. She had seen a boy drown and be saved. She had seen a web of light connecting everyone to everyone. She had seen an angel who was not an angel as she had ever imagined one, not the kind on Christmas cards, not the gold-leafed figures in her mother's Bible, but something tired and grieving and patient and real.

"There were lights," she said again, because it was the only sentence her throat could manage and because it was true.

"That's very common," the doctor said gently. "A lot of patients report that."

Rosa wanted to say: *Not like this. Not lights like lamps, not lights like the sun. A living light. A web. A pattern I could see but not read.* She wanted to say: *I saw a boy who looks like my husband and he will be important, I know it the way I know my own name, and there was an angel watching him, watching everything, and the angel was so tired, so old, so heavy with something I cannot describe, and I need you to understand that what I saw was real.*

What came out was: "There were lights."

The doctor patted her hand. "Try to rest, Mrs. Chen."

Rosa closed her eyes. Behind them, the afterimage of the web pulsed once, like a heartbeat, and then went dark.

---

Her daughter arrived at dawn.

Lily was thirty-five, a real estate agent in Flushing, practical and sharp-cornered in the way daughters of immigrant mothers become when they have spent their whole lives translating between two worlds. She brought a thermos of congee and a change of clothes and an expression Rosa knew well — the particular tight-lipped set of Lily's mouth when she was frightened but had decided not to let it show.

"Mama," she said, sitting on the edge of the bed. "The doctor says you died."

"I know."

"Eighteen minutes."

"I know."

Lily unscrewed the thermos. The congee was plain — ginger and salt, the way Rosa had made it for Lily when she was sick as a child. Lily had learned the recipe not from instruction but from proximity, the slow osmosis of a shared kitchen.

"They want you on a new diet," Lily said. "Pacemaker. Physical therapy. The doctor says — "

"I saw things," Rosa said.

Lily held the spoon to her mother's lips. "Eat first."

---

Over the weeks that followed, Rosa tried.

She tried in the hospital, propped against starched pillows, her chest stapled and stitched and aching. She tried at Lily's house in Bayside, where she had been moved after the doctors said she could not live alone. She tried at the kitchen table, on the porch, in the garden where Lily's husband, James, smoked his evening cigarettes and pretended he didn't.

The problem was language. Rosa had spent thirty-eight years as an elementary school teacher. She had taught children the alphabet. She had taught them to spell *because* and *beautiful* and *believe*. She knew words the way a carpenter knows wood — intimately, structurally, by the grain. And she could not find the right ones.

"I saw a boy," she told Lily one evening in April. They were shelling peas on the porch. The air smelled like cut grass and the faint chemical sweetness of the neighbor's azaleas.

"Mama."

"A boy who looked like your father. He was drowning. In a pool, I think. A swimming pool. And another boy pulled him out."

"The doctor said you might have hallucinations. It's normal after — "

"I wasn't hallucinating." Rosa cracked a pea pod with her thumb. Inside, the peas were bright, tightly packed, perfect. "It was different. I can't explain how. But it was different."

Lily shelled peas and said nothing.

"And I saw him older," Rosa said. "The same boy, grown up. He was building something. Working with some kind of thin material, like fabric but not fabric. He was in a room full of machines."

"Mama, eat your peas."

"And there was an angel."

A pause. Lily set down her bowl and turned to face her mother. She had her father's face — the same long jaw, the same flat patience in the eyes. Rosa had loved that face across twenty-seven years of marriage, and she loved it now in its daughter-shaped echo.

"What did the angel look like?" Lily asked. Humoring her. Rosa could hear it in the careful, even tone — the same voice Lily used with difficult clients, the voice that said *I am listening* and meant *I am waiting for you to finish*.

"Sad," Rosa said. "Very old and very sad. Not a person. More like — " She turned the pea pod in her hands. "Like a place where light has been sitting for too long. Like grief that learned to stand up."

Lily picked up her bowl again. "Yes, Mama."

---

In May, Rosa told Lily about the pattern.

They were in the kitchen. Lily was steaming fish; Rosa was sitting at the table because she was no longer allowed to stand for long periods. Her chest ached in the mornings and the evenings now, a low structural complaint, as if the doctors had rebuilt her heart from slightly wrong materials.

"It wasn't random," Rosa said. "What I saw. It was all connected. Like lights on a Christmas tree — each one separate, but all on the same wire."

"We need more soy sauce," Lily said.

"The boy drowning, and the boy being saved, and the boy in the laboratory — they're the same boy. Different ages. I'm sure of it."

"The fish is almost done."

"Lily. Listen to me. I think he's family."

Lily set down her spatula. Rosa could see the effort it cost — the setting-aside of the fish, the soy sauce, the evening routine — to stand still and listen to a story that made no sense.

"I'm listening, Mama."

"I think he's a grandson. Or a great-grandson. Someone who hasn't been born yet."

"Mama, I'm not even pregnant."

"I know that."

"James and I aren't — "

"I *know* that." Rosa folded her hands on the table. Her fingers were thin, veined, spotted with age. They had graded ten thousand spelling tests. They had wiped ten thousand noses. They had held small hands across busy streets and braided hair and tied shoes and pressed Band-Aids onto scraped knees with the calm authority of a woman who had spent her whole life taking care of other people's children because the world was full of children who needed taking care of. "I don't need you to believe me. I need you to remember."

Lily looked at her mother for a long time. Outside, James's cigarette smoke drifted past the kitchen window.

"I'll remember," she said.

---

June. July. The days lengthened and the heat settled over Queens like a hand pressing down. Rosa sat in Lily's garden and watched the tomato plants grow. She had always kept a garden — at the old house in Flushing, before the landlord sold, she had grown tomatoes and long beans and, one August, a single watermelon that split in the heat and fed every child on the block. Now she watched Lily's garden and offered advice Lily did not take and felt, with each passing day, the slow recession of her own vitality, a tide going out.

The pacemaker ticked faithfully in her chest. The rest of her was winding down.

She dreamed, sometimes, of the light. She woke with her hands reaching for something that was not there.

She told Lily about the angel five more times. Each telling caught a different facet — not because Rosa was inventing, but because the vision was larger than her memory, and each approach to it revealed a new surface, the way a diamond looks different depending on which face you hold to the light. She told Lily about the woman in the yellow headscarf. She told her about an old man laughing. She told her about a web of connections she could see but not follow.

Once, in August, she said: "I think I taught one of them. One of the people in the vision. A long time ago."

"One of your students?"

"Maybe. I can't remember. There were so many." Thirty-eight years of second-graders. Hundreds of children passing through her classroom, briefly hers, then gone. She remembered some clearly — Bobby Fong with his misspellings, Maria Santos who could multiply three-digit numbers in her head, a skinny blond boy with a stammer whose name she could not recall — and the rest had faded into the general blur of small faces and small hands and the smell of paste and pencil shavings and that particular clean-scalp scent that belonged to children and to no one else.

She did not remember Thomas Reed. He had been in her class for one year — 1962 — a quiet boy, serious, who sat in the third row and did his homework neatly and never caused trouble. He had passed through her classroom the way water passes through a sieve.

Thomas Reed did not remember her, either. He had been seven. His teacher had been a young Chinese-American woman who smelled like chalk and called him *Thomas, dear* and who had once let him stay inside during recess to read because it was raining and he did not have a raincoat. He did not remember this. It was filed in the deep sediment of early childhood, as lost as the taste of his first birthday cake.

They had shared a year. In that year, Rosa had taught Thomas Reed to spell *because* and *beautiful* and *believe*. Decades later, Thomas Reed would become a prosecutor, and he would send a boy named David Okonkwo to prison for ten years, and David Okonkwo, at the age of twelve, would pull a drowning child from a swimming pool — a boy named Marcus Chen, Rosa's grandson, the child she had seen in her vision, the child who had not yet been born.

But Rosa did not know any of this. She saw fragments of a pattern and could not see the whole. She held pieces of a future that was, to her, only light and faces and a feeling of vast, aching significance that she could not translate into anything her daughter would accept.

---

September came. The tomatoes ripened and split on the vine. The air turned sharp at the edges.

On September 8th, Lily found her mother in the garden, sitting in the folding chair with a blanket over her knees, watching a cardinal on the fence post.

"Come inside, Mama. It's getting cold."

"Not yet." Rosa watched the cardinal. It was male, bright red, its crest lifted. She had always liked cardinals. There was something defiant about them — that refusal to be dull, that insistence on color even in the gray months. "Lily."

"Yes, Mama."

"The boy I saw. The one who hasn't been born yet."

"Mama — "

"The angels will watch him. Remember that."

Lily crouched beside her mother's chair. In eleven years, she would have a son. She would name him Marcus, after her mother's father. She did not know any of this. She was just a woman kneeling in a garden, holding her mother's hand, listening to words that made no sense.

"Okay, Mama."

"The boy who hasn't been born yet — the angels will watch him. Promise me you'll remember."

"I promise."

Rosa nodded. The cardinal took flight. The sky over Queens was the color of a bruise fading to yellow, and from somewhere down the block came the sound of a car alarm, that two-note wail that was as much a part of the neighborhood as the subway rumble and the distant horns, and from the kitchen the smell of rice cooking, and Rosa thought: *This is enough. This is a good place to stop.*

---

She died on September 22nd, 1985, six months and eight days after her heart had stopped and been restarted in a room she would never return to.

She died in Lily's guest bedroom, in a single bed with a brass frame, with the window open because she liked the air even when it was cold. Lily was beside her. James was downstairs. The pacemaker in Rosa's chest continued its measured beating for eleven minutes after the rest of her had gone, like a clock ticking in an empty house.

Lily held her mother's hand until it cooled. Then she closed the window and went downstairs.

---

The story did not die with Rosa.

Lily carried it the way families carry stories — compressed, simplified, shorn of its strangeness and gaining sentiment with each retelling. She told it to Robert, the man she married after her divorce from James, on their fourth date, as a funny-sad thing her mother had said toward the end.

"She said she saw angels," Lily said over dinner. "She said they were going to watch her grandson."

"Did she have a grandson?"

"No. I wasn't even pregnant."

They laughed. They ordered dessert.

But the story lodged. It became a family artifact — not quite believed, not quite dismissed, carried forward the way all family stories are carried forward, losing specificity and gaining weight with each generation.

When Marcus was born in 1996 — eleven years after Rosa's death — Lily looked at her son's face, red, scrunched, furious at the cold bright world, and thought of her mother. She thought: *Is this the one you saw?*

She did not say this out loud.

When Marcus was eight and nearly drowned at a community pool in the Bronx — when a twelve-year-old boy she had never seen before dove in and hauled her son to the surface, coughing and alive — Lily stood at the pool's edge with a towel and her heart hammering in her ears and thought of her mother again.

"Your grandmother said the angels would watch you," she told Marcus that night, tucking him into bed. His hair was still damp. He smelled like chlorine.

"I don't have a grandmother," Marcus said. He was eight. He was literal.

"You did. She died before you were born. She said the angels would help you."

Marcus considered this with the grave seriousness of an eight-year-old evaluating an unlikely claim. "Was it an angel that pulled me out of the water?"

"No, baby. It was a boy."

"Then it wasn't an angel."

Lily smoothed his hair. "Go to sleep."

Marcus filed the story in the place children file stories that do not make sense — somewhere deep and inaccessible, beside fairy tales and the sounds you hear after the lights go out. It was still there years later, when he was twenty-one and struggling through a PhD he was not sure he could finish, and twenty-eight and standing on a balcony with an acceptance letter to a consulting firm in one hand and his life's work in the other. It surfaced at odd moments, like a coin catching light at the bottom of a fountain: *Your grandmother said the angels would watch you.*

He never believed it.

He never quite forgot it, either.

And Rosa — who had taught second grade for thirty-eight years, who had graded ten thousand spelling tests, who had seen something she could not explain and spent her last six months trying to hand it to someone who could carry it — Rosa had done what she could.

She had planted the seed.

She could not know that one of her second-graders would become a prosecutor who would send a boy to prison for ten years. She could not know that the boy in the pool and the boy in the vision were the same person — her own grandson, the child she saw drowning and being saved. She could not know that the tired angel in the light was, at this very moment, sitting in a waiting room down the hall from where Rosa had died and been reborn, looking as ordinary and as worn-down as anyone.

She had seen the pattern. She could not read it.

But she passed it on.
