# Chapter 6: The Twenty-Dollar Bill

**Baltimore, Maryland — October 1991**

---

The cereal tasted like wet cardboard. Keisha ate it anyway because it was breakfast and you ate breakfast. That was a rule. Her mother had rules about everything -- brush your teeth, lock the door, eat breakfast before school, homework before the TV goes on. The rules kept going even when things stopped making sense, and Keisha liked that about them. The rules meant the day had a shape.

She was eating Cheerios with water because the milk had been gone since Tuesday.

The kitchen smelled like Fabuloso, the purple kind in the tall bottle. Her mother had mopped the floor before leaving for her first job, the register at the Rite Aid on North Avenue. The counter was wiped. The stovetop was clean. Everything in the kitchen was clean because that was another rule, maybe the biggest one -- you kept things clean even when you couldn't keep them full.

A sound from the front of the apartment. Not the front door -- the bedroom. Her parents' bedroom. Keisha stopped chewing. She heard drawers opening, the metal scrape of hangers across the closet rod, something heavy dropped on the bed. She knew what packing sounded like. Her mother packed for work every morning: uniform in the bag, name tag clipped on, shoes by the door.

This was different. This was her father packing, and her father didn't work mornings.

She carried her bowl to the sink and climbed the stairs. The bedroom door was open. Her father stood over a duffel bag on the bed, pushing shirts into it. Not folding. Pushing. He had on his good jacket, the brown one, and his boots -- the ones with the heavy soles that he didn't wear to go to the store.

She stood in the doorway and watched him. He didn't see her for a few seconds, and in those seconds she saw his face the way you see a person's face when they don't know you're looking. He looked tired. Not the angry-tired from when he and her mother argued about money through the wall at night. Just tired. Like he was done with something and the doneness had settled into his bones.

He saw her. His hands stopped moving.

"Hey, baby girl."

"Where you going?"

He looked at the bag. He looked at Keisha. He zipped it shut.

"Daddy's got to go handle some things."

Keisha knew what that meant. She was nine, not stupid. Trina Parker's father had left last spring with the same kind of bag, and Trina said he was coming back. He didn't come back. Trina stopped talking about him by summer.

"When you coming back?"

Her father picked up the bag. He came over and put his hand on the top of her head the way he did when she was little. His palm was warm and heavy and she could smell his aftershave, the green bottle that sat on the shelf above the toilet.

"Soon," he said.

He went down the stairs. Keisha followed him to the front window in her bedroom and knelt on her bed with her face close to the glass. Her breath made a circle of fog and she wiped it with her sleeve.

A car was parked at the curb. Red, two-door, a car she had never seen before. Someone sat in the driver's seat but the windshield glare hid their face. Her father walked down the steps and put the bag in the back seat. He stood there for a moment with his hand on the open door, and Keisha pressed her fingers against the window. He could look up right now. He could see her hand on the glass and come back up the steps and push those shirts back into the drawers.

He got in. The car pulled away down Ashland Avenue, turned the corner, and was gone.

Keisha stayed at the window until the shadow of the row house across the street reached the middle of the road. She wasn't crying. It seemed like the kind of thing you should cry about, but nothing came. She felt cold, the same cold as the apartment, starting behind her chest and spreading into her arms and legs until she felt like a house with the heat turned off.

She went downstairs. She washed the bowl and set it in the rack. She got her backpack and locked the door behind her, checking the knob twice the way her mother had taught her.

---

Three days later, walking home from school, Keisha found the money.

It was a Thursday. The trees on her block had gone orange and brown and the leaves piled in the gutters and against the chain-link fences. Wind had a bite. Keisha's jacket was from last year and the sleeves didn't reach her wrists anymore, so she walked with her hands pushed deep in her pockets and her chin tucked down. She could hear a basketball game two blocks over -- the thump of the ball on asphalt, somebody laughing, the chain net at the rec center making that zipper sound when the ball went through clean.

She walked this route every day. Knew every crack and buckle in the sidewalk, knew which houses had dogs that barked and which had old people who sat behind their screen doors and watched the street like television. Mrs. Delaney on the corner had a ceramic frog on her stoop that Keisha touched every morning for luck, though she had never told anyone about this and would have denied it if asked. Mr. Patterson kept his TV on all day and she could hear it through his window -- the theme songs of game shows, the ticking of the clock on the news.

Today she was looking down because of the wind, and she saw it.

A twenty-dollar bill, folded once, lying on the sidewalk right where the concrete buckled near the Pattersons' front steps. Not crumpled like somebody dropped it. Not wet or dirty or trampled into a crack. Just sitting there, folded in half, Andrew Jackson's face staring up at the sky. Her mother had taught her the presidents on the money -- Washington on the one, Lincoln on the five, Jackson on the twenty, though her mother said Jackson wasn't anybody worth admiring.

Keisha stopped walking. She looked up the street. Down the street. Nobody. Mr. Patterson's TV flickered blue through his window. Two houses down, a cat sat on a marble stoop and watched her with its head tilted. No one was checking their pockets. No one was looking for anything they'd lost.

She picked it up.

The bill was dry. It hadn't rained since Monday but the gutters were still damp, the leaves in the gutter still wet. This bill was dry and clean, like it had just been set down. She unfolded it and held it flat between her palms. Twenty dollars. She had never held a twenty-dollar bill before. She'd held the fives her mother gave her for the store, and once a ten at her aunt's wedding last year in Dundalk. But a twenty was different. A twenty was heavy in a way that had nothing to do with weight.

She folded it again and put it in her pocket, closed her hand around it. The paper was already warm from her skin.

Something passed over her. Not a shadow -- the sky was overcast, no sun to cast one. More like the feeling of someone standing behind you in a line, the warmth of another body close to yours. She looked around. The street was empty. The cat on the stoop had gone. The wind pushed a candy wrapper across the pavement.

But the warmth stayed. A small heat at the back of her neck, like a hand held an inch from her skin. It was there and then it wasn't, the way a smell comes through an open window and is gone before you can name it.

Keisha shivered. Not from cold. She didn't know what from. She stood on the buckled sidewalk with the twenty dollars in her pocket and felt -- she didn't have a word for it. Felt noticed. The way you feel noticed when a teacher calls on you and you didn't raise your hand, except there was no teacher and no classroom, just the street and the wind and the leaves and the strange, brief sense that something was paying attention.

She shook it off the way you shake off a chill. Kept walking. Her sneakers scuffed the sidewalk and the sound was ordinary and the feeling faded, and by the time she reached the corner she'd almost convinced herself she'd imagined it.

Almost.

---

She thought about candy. She was nine years old and she had twenty dollars and the corner store was right there, the one with the bell on the door and the glass case full of Jolly Ranchers and Swedish Fish and those wax bottles with the colored juice inside. Twenty dollars of candy was a kingdom. Twenty dollars of candy was the kind of thing you dreamed about and described to your friends in the cafeteria with your eyes wide: *and then I got a whole bag of Sour Punch Straws and two packs of Bubble Yum and I still had money left over.*

She stood on the corner and looked at the store. Through the window she could see Mr. Kim behind the register, the same Orioles cap he always wore pushed back on his head.

But the candy thought had something behind it, the way a nice picture in a frame sometimes has a water stain on the wall behind it. What was behind the candy was the kitchen. The empty pantry. The box of Cheerios that was almost gone. The refrigerator with no milk and a jar of yellow mustard and a bottle of hot sauce and nothing else. Her mother's face at six in the morning, standing at the stove boiling water for instant coffee before the first of three jobs.

Three jobs. The register at Rite Aid. The cleaning company in the evening, offices downtown, riding the bus in a polo shirt with somebody else's name on the breast pocket. And Saturday mornings at the church, setting up folding chairs and wiping down tables in the fellowship hall, which wasn't really a job because they paid her in grocery bags from the food bank, but her mother counted it as a job because the food was real.

Three jobs and there was no milk.

Keisha walked past the corner store. The bell on the door didn't ring because she didn't go in. She turned left on Eager Street and walked two more blocks to the Food Lion, where the prices were lower and you could buy store brand if you knew what you were looking for.

---

The Food Lion smelled like floor cleaner and cold air. Keisha picked up a basket and walked the aisles the way her mother did, slow and counting. She had never done this alone before, but she'd watched it enough times that her body knew the pattern: start with the back wall where the dairy was, then the bread aisle, then the middle aisles for peanut butter and rice, then the register.

Milk, store brand, a dollar thirty-nine. She picked up the gallon and put it in the basket and the weight of it felt like something, like an answer to a question she hadn't known she was asking. Bread, eighty-nine cents, the kind in the yellow wrapper. Peanut butter, the big jar, two dollars and nineteen cents. She stood in front of the eggs for a long time, reading the prices the way her mother read them -- not just the number on the sticker but the math behind it, the cost per meal, the days it would stretch. A dozen eggs, a dollar fifty-nine. Twelve eggs. If she and her mother each had two eggs for breakfast, that was three days. If she had one and her mother had two, that was four.

She put the eggs in the basket.

She kept counting. Six thirty-six. She had twenty dollars. She could get more. Rice, the two-pound bag, a dollar twenty-nine. A can of black beans, sixty-nine cents. A bar of Ivory soap because they were almost out and her mother would need it. Seventy-nine cents. She stood in front of the American cheese -- singles, a dollar twenty-nine -- and picked up the package, put it down, picked it up again.

Nine thirteen.

She carried the basket to the register. The woman there had long nails painted coral and a name tag that said DONNA. Donna looked at Keisha. Looked at the groceries. Looked at Keisha standing there alone, no parent in sight, a nine-year-old girl buying a week's worth of food with a twenty-dollar bill.

"That everything, hon?"

"Yes ma'am."

Donna rang it up. The total was nine dollars and forty-seven cents with tax. Keisha put the twenty on the counter and Donna made change: a ten, a quarter, a nickel, and twenty-three pennies because the register was low on dimes. Keisha put the change in her pocket, each coin separate and counted.

She packed the groceries into her backpack herself. The milk went in last, heavy and cold against her spine through the nylon. She zipped it up and put her arms through the straps and felt the weight pull at her shoulders and it was good, the heaviness. Like carrying something that mattered.

---

Walking home, she passed a boy sitting on the bottom step of a row house on Collington Avenue. Little -- maybe six. He was hugging his knees and his shoulders were shaking with the kind of crying that comes out quiet, the kind you do when you've been at it a while and all the loud parts are used up. He had on a coat that was too big, the sleeves folded back twice, and his sneakers were torn. Not scuffed-up torn. Done. The sole of the left one had separated from the canvas and hung open like a jaw, showing his sock underneath.

Keisha stopped. The boy didn't look up. A car went past on the cross street with the bass turned up so loud she felt it in her sternum.

She didn't know this boy. He was too small for her school. But she knew the crying. She'd heard it through her bedroom wall some nights, her mother in the dark when she thought Keisha was asleep, crying the way people cry when they're not sad about one thing but about everything, about the weight of everything.

She reached into her pocket and felt the change. Ten dollars and fifty-three cents. Her mother would need it. The electric bill was due and the phone bill was due and the rent was the fifteenth and every dollar was already spent twice.

She pulled out the ten.

It wasn't a decision. Not the way adults decide things, weighing and measuring, turning the options over. It was something simpler. It was what water does when it reaches a slope -- it goes where it has to go.

"Hey."

The boy looked up. His face was wet and his nose was running and his eyes were the kind of swollen that comes from rubbing.

"What's wrong?"

"My shoe broke." He sniffed hard and looked down at it. "My mama said she can't buy new shoes till she gets paid."

Keisha looked at the shoe. It was done. She knew because she'd tried to fix her own shoes with duct tape last winter and the sidewalk ate through the tape in two days.

"Here," she said. She held out the ten. "For shoes."

He stared at it. "For real?"

"Take it before I change my mind."

He took it with both hands, the way you hold something that might disappear. "Thank you," he said, and his voice cracked on it.

"Go home," Keisha said. "Tell your mama."

She turned and kept walking. She didn't look back because looking back would make it into something bigger than it was. It was just money. She still had fifty-three cents and a backpack full of groceries and that was enough.

Halfway down the block, the feeling came again. Warmth. Not sun-warmth -- the day was grey and getting greyer. A warmth that seemed to come from inside the air itself, or from some direction she couldn't point to, the way you can feel a radiator in a room without seeing it. It settled across her shoulders like a coat being draped there, and for three or four steps Keisha walked inside it, this strange pocket of warmth on a cold October street, and she was not afraid.

Then it was gone. Just the wind again, just the weight of the backpack, just her sneakers on the cracked sidewalk. She kept walking. She didn't look around this time. There was nothing to see. But something in her -- some quiet part below thought, below words -- knew the warmth had been real. Knew it the way she knew her own name, without evidence, without reason. Just knowledge, sitting there in her chest like a stone in a creek bed, smooth and heavy and immovable.

She reached her stoop. The crack in the top step. The screen door with its duct-tape patch. She unlocked the door, checked the knob twice, and went inside.

---

She put the milk in the refrigerator. The eggs on the shelf beside it. The peanut butter and bread and rice and beans on the counter, lined up neat, labels facing out. The soap she set on the edge of the kitchen sink. When she was done she stood back and looked at it and something expanded in her chest that she didn't have a name for. Not pride exactly. Something closer to the feeling of a finished drawing, when you step back and see that the lines add up to something that looks like what you meant.

She made herself a peanut butter sandwich. Two slices of bread, the peanut butter spread thick because she could, because there was a whole jar of it. She sat at the kitchen table and ate it and it tasted like the best thing she'd ever eaten, and she knew that wasn't true -- her mother's fried chicken was the best, everybody said so -- but the sandwich had something the chicken didn't. She had bought it. She had carried it home on her back. It was hers in a way that food had never been hers before, and the ownership made it sweet.

The apartment was quiet. The clock on the wall above the stove ticked. Somewhere outside a dog barked and stopped. The radiator clanked once, the pipes complaining the way they did when the building was cold, though no heat came out -- the heat wouldn't come on until November, which was the rule in the lease, and the landlord followed that rule even when October turned bitter.

Keisha washed her plate and dried it and put it away. She did her homework at the kitchen table -- long division, a worksheet on the water cycle, twenty spelling words she already knew because she read two grades above her level, had read two grades above since she was seven. Her mother took her to the Pratt Library on Saturdays and let her check out six books at a time, the maximum, and Keisha always finished them by Wednesday and spent Thursday and Friday rereading the best one. This week it was *Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry*, and she'd read it twice already, and parts of it made her so angry she had to put it down and walk around the kitchen before she could keep going, which was a new feeling about a book and one she wasn't sure she liked but kept coming back to anyway.

She finished her homework and put it in her folder and put the folder in her backpack by the door. She turned on the television and watched the last fifteen minutes of *A Different World* and then the news came on and she turned it off because the news made the apartment feel lonelier.

---

Her mother came home at 11:07 p.m.

Keisha was supposed to be asleep. She was in bed in the dark with her eyes open, listening for the sounds that meant the day was over: key in the lock. Door. The sigh her mother made when she sat on the bottom step to take off her shoes, a sound that came from somewhere deeper than her lungs. The tap running because she always drank a glass of water first, standing at the sink in the dark, before she did anything else.

Tonight the pattern broke. After the water, a pause. A long pause. Then the sound of the refrigerator door opening, the seal pulling apart with that soft pop.

Silence.

Keisha slid out of bed and went to the top of the stairs. From there she could see the edge of the kitchen -- the yellow light from the ceiling fixture with one dead bulb, the cracked linoleum, the table where she'd done her homework. Her mother stood in front of the open refrigerator with one shoe on and one shoe off. She was looking at the milk. The eggs. Her hand came up slowly and rested on the gallon like she was checking if it was real.

She turned and saw the counter. The bread. The peanut butter. The rice and beans. The bar of soap on the edge of the sink.

Her mother put her hand over her mouth.

Keisha came down the stairs. Her nightgown brushed the backs of her knees. Her mother turned around and her eyes were wet, the light from the refrigerator behind her making her look like a photograph, the edges bright and the middle in shadow.

"Keisha Marie Williams."

"Yes ma'am."

"Where did you get this?"

"I found money. On the sidewalk."

"How much money?"

"Twenty dollars."

Her mother looked at the counter. Counted the items the way she counted everything -- with her lips barely moving, the adding happening somewhere behind her eyes.

"This isn't twenty dollars' worth of groceries."

"I gave some to a boy," Keisha said. "His shoes were broken."

Her mother was quiet. She stood there with one shoe on, her cleaning-company polo still tucked into her khakis, her hair pulled back in the bun she wore for work. Her hands hung at her sides. She was looking at Keisha the way you look at a thing you didn't expect to find -- not shocked, not confused, but rearranged. As though some picture she'd been carrying had just shifted, and the new version was sharper than the old one.

She sat down in the kitchen chair. She put her hands in her lap, one on top of the other, and she cried.

It was quiet crying. No sound except her breathing. Her shoulders moved and her mouth pressed tight and the tears came down her face and caught the light from the overhead bulb, and Keisha stood there in her nightgown and felt something she couldn't name. A kind of scared that wasn't about danger. Her mother was the person who fixed things. Her mother got up every morning and stood behind a register and mopped floors and set up folding chairs and came home and washed her uniform and did it again. Her mother said *we're fine* and *everything's going to work out* and *I got this, baby,* and Keisha had believed her every time because what else was there to believe.

Her mother didn't cry. And now she was crying, and it was because Keisha had bought groceries, and that didn't make sense.

"Mama? Are you sad?"

Her mother wiped her eyes with the back of her hand, the way Keisha did, the same gesture, and Keisha noticed this for the first time -- that she moved like her mother. That some things you don't learn. You inherit them through the skin.

"No, baby. I'm not sad."

"Then why are you crying?"

"Because you're good." Her mother's voice broke on the word, and she pressed her lips together and breathed through her nose until she could talk again. "Because you're nine years old and you bought groceries and you gave money to a child you don't even know, and that means I must be doing something right. And today I needed to know that."

She reached out and pulled Keisha in. Keisha's face pressed against the stiff polo that smelled like the offices her mother cleaned -- Windex and carpet and the particular staleness of air conditioning after hours. Under that, the tired warmth of her mother's skin. Her mother had gotten thinner. The bones of her shoulder pressed through the fabric. When had her mother gotten thinner?

They stayed like that. The refrigerator hummed. The clock ticked. The apartment was cold but her mother was warm, and the cold didn't matter.

After a while her mother leaned back and held Keisha's face in both hands and looked at her. Looked at her the way she looked at the bills when she was doing math -- studying, figuring, making it add up.

"You know what your grandmother used to say?"

"Which thing?"

Her mother smiled. It was a tired smile with a crack running through it, but it was real.

"She used to say -- when you find what you need, exactly when you need it, that's God keeping accounts."

Keisha thought about that. The twenty-dollar bill, dry on the damp sidewalk. The fold. The placement. The warmth she had felt and could not explain, twice, like someone standing close who wasn't there.

"You think God put that money there?"

Her mother let go of her face and leaned back in the chair. She looked at the groceries on the counter, then at the ceiling, then at Keisha.

"I think something did," she said. "I don't know what to call it. But your grandmother would say it's the same thing that makes sure the person who needs help and the person who can give it end up on the same sidewalk at the same time."

Keisha didn't know what to do with that. She was nine. God was a word from church, a voice in the songs her mother played on Sunday mornings, a face in the picture above her grandmother's bed -- an old white man on a cloud, which even at nine she thought was probably wrong but didn't have a better picture to replace it with. She didn't think God put twenty-dollar bills on sidewalks. That seemed like a small thing for God to do.

But the warmth. She kept coming back to the warmth. That pocket of heat on the cold street, draped over her shoulders like something placed there. That wasn't wind. That wasn't imagination. That was real the way the concrete under her feet was real, the way her mother's hands on her face were real.

"Go to bed, baby," her mother said. "You got school in the morning."

"Mama?"

"Hmm?"

"We got eggs. For breakfast."

Her mother looked at her. The tired smile came back, and this time the crack in it was smaller.

"Yeah," she said. "We do."

---

In bed, Keisha lay on her back and traced the crack in the ceiling with her eyes. The crack looked like a river if you squinted, branching and narrowing, and she followed it from one wall nearly to the other, imagining tiny boats on it, people traveling from one side of the ceiling to the other on a river no one else could see.

She thought about her father. Not the leaving itself -- she wasn't ready to look at that straight on, the way you can't look at the sun. She thought about it sideways. The brown jacket. The sound the front door made when he closed it, soft, barely a click, like he was trying not to wake someone. The red car. The person in the driver's seat she couldn't see.

She didn't hate him. She didn't know what she felt. It was like a room she hadn't been into yet, a door inside her that was closed, and she knew she'd have to open it eventually but not tonight. Tonight she was tired and her mother was home and there was milk in the refrigerator and eggs for the morning, and those things were enough to hold the door shut a little longer.

She thought about the boy on the steps. Whether ten dollars was enough for shoes. Whether he'd told his mother yet, and whether his mother had cried too, and whether crying because your child did something good was a thing all mothers did or just hers.

She thought about the money. Where it came from. People dropped money sometimes -- she'd found a quarter once, and a dime, and one time a wadded-up dollar near the school parking lot. But this was different. This was twenty dollars, clean and dry, sitting on the damp concrete like it had been placed there. Set down with intention. Meant.

*That's God keeping accounts.*

She didn't know about God. She was nine and she knew the things nine-year-olds know, which are mostly the things that are right in front of them: the temperature of the apartment, the taste of peanut butter on bread she bought herself, the feeling of her mother's arms, the fact that her father was gone and there was no sound from his side of the bed when she pressed her ear to the wall.

But she knew the warmth was real. And she knew what she'd done with the money was right -- not because anyone told her, not because of the rules, but because something in her had pointed at the boy on the steps and said *there* the way a compass says north. Not a voice. Not a thought. Something quieter than both. A direction she hadn't chosen and couldn't explain but trusted the way you trust gravity -- not because you understand it but because it's never been wrong.

She turned on her side. The apartment was quiet except for the refrigerator and the pipes and her mother moving around in the bathroom -- toothbrush, water, the creak of the medicine cabinet. Ordinary sounds. The sounds of a day ending the way it was supposed to end, with the doors locked and the lights off and someone you loved on the other side of the wall.

Tomorrow there would be breakfast with milk. Eggs, scrambled, the way her mother made them, with hot sauce. She would walk to school on the same cracked sidewalk, past Mrs. Delaney's ceramic frog, past Mr. Patterson's blue-lit window. Her father would not be there. And that was how it was going to be now. That was the new shape of things.

She thought: *Maybe he'll come back.*

She thought it the way you toss a penny into a fountain -- not because you believe it works, but because it costs almost nothing to hope.

Outside, the row houses of Ashland Avenue stood shoulder to shoulder in the dark, red brick gone black under the streetlights, the marble stoops glowing faintly white. Somewhere a dog barked twice and stopped. A siren rose and faded on the far side of the neighborhood. The city breathed around her -- trash and cooking grease and October leaves and the mineral smell of old brick and, underneath it all, the deep patient scent of the harbor that found its way up the streets and through the screens and settled into everything, so that even the blankets smelled faintly of salt.

Keisha Williams, age nine, slept. And whatever watched over her -- God or grace or the strange geometry that puts a twenty-dollar bill in the path of a hungry child who will give half of it away -- kept its own counsel, and kept its accounts, and said nothing.
