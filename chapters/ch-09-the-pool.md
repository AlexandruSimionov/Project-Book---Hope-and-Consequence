# Chapter 9: The Pool

**Summer 2004 — Prospect Park Public Pool, Brooklyn, New York**

---

The water was a lie. It looked like it would be cold and it wasn't. It looked like it would be clean and it wasn't that either. Marcus Chen stood at the edge of the shallow end with his toes curled over the concrete lip and stared down at the surface, which was bright and flat and held the sun inside it like something trapped, and the water looked like a window into another world, a blue one, a world where everything was slow and quiet and nothing could touch you.

He was eight years old, and he had been thinking about windows a lot.

His mother had dropped him off twenty minutes ago. She had parked the Corolla in the lot by the chain-link fence and walked him to the gate and paid the three dollars and crouched down so their eyes were level, her hands on his shoulders, her face doing the thing it did when she wanted him to understand that what she was saying was not a suggestion.

"Stay in the shallow end. I'll be back at three."

"Okay."

"Marcus. The shallow end."

"I said okay."

She had looked at him a moment longer, the way she sometimes did, as if she were reading something written on him that he could not see. Then she kissed his forehead and stood up and walked back through the gate, and he watched her through the chain-link as she got into the Corolla and pulled out of the lot, the car making the sound it always made when it shifted gears, a grinding complaint that his mother pretended not to hear.

She had to work. She always had to work. She cleaned offices downtown on Saturdays, taking the 7 train into Manhattan and coming back smelling of Windex and the chemical soap in the dispensers in bathrooms that were nicer than any room in their apartment. She worked because his father was gone and because the rent was due on the first and because the world, as she had explained to Marcus once with a precision that startled him, did not give you things for free. She worked and he waited, and in the space between her leaving and her coming back, Marcus occupied himself with books and sidewalks and the particular solitude of a kid who was comfortable alone but not entirely sure he was supposed to be.

The pool was not his idea. His mother's friend Mrs. Reyes had told her about it, had said it was safe, had said there was a lifeguard, had said the neighborhood kids went there all summer. His mother had been skeptical. Marcus had been skeptical too, though he hadn't said so, because he'd learned that the word *skeptical* made adults look at him strangely, as if an eight-year-old who used words like that was performing a trick, and Marcus did not like being looked at as if he were performing.

He was not a strong swimmer. His mother had taken him to the Y twice the previous winter, and both times he had stood in the shallow end and practiced putting his face in the water while a teenager in a red swimsuit told him to blow bubbles. He had blown bubbles. He had not progressed beyond bubbles.

Now he stood at the edge of the pool and watched the other kids. There were maybe twenty of them, spread across the water in loose clusters, and the noise they made was enormous — screaming, laughing, splashing, the percussive slap of bodies hitting the surface, the high shriek of a girl being dunked, the low stupid laughter of the boys doing the dunking. The sound bounced off the concrete deck and the chain-link fence and the flat brick wall of the community center and came back changed, distorted, a wall of noise that made Marcus's teeth ache.

He stepped into the water. It came up to his waist and it was warm, the warmth of something that had been sitting in the sun for a long time, and it smelled like chlorine — sharp, medical, the smell of things being killed. He waded forward, his feet finding the bottom, which was rough and painted blue and scattered with the ghosts of Band-Aids. He kept his hands above the surface. He moved slowly because moving slowly was how Marcus moved through most things, and because the water was louder than he'd expected, the sound of it against his body a constant low pressure that made him feel surrounded.

A boy cannonballed in three feet away. The splash hit Marcus in the face and he flinched and stepped backward, his heel catching on the slope of the pool floor where the shallow end began its descent toward the deep. He caught himself. His heart was going fast. The boy surfaced, laughing, and swam away without looking at him.

Marcus moved along the wall of the pool, one hand trailing against the tile, keeping to the edge where the water was shallow and where the other kids were not. The tile was slippery with algae or something like it, a faint green film that his fingers passed through. He watched the water line on the wall — there was a dark band where the water had been higher, and above it the tile was dry and hot, and below it the tile was submerged and slick, and Marcus thought about how everything had two versions of itself, a dry version and a wet version, and how you couldn't tell from looking at the dry version what the wet version would be like.

The roughhousing was getting closer. Three older boys — ten, maybe eleven — were wrestling near the center of the pool, their arms locked around each other's necks, their bodies thrashing, and the waves they made pushed out in concentric rings that rocked Marcus against the wall. He moved farther along the edge, toward the corner where the shallow end met the rope that marked the boundary of the deep end. The rope was a line of white and blue floats strung on a cable, and it sagged in the middle where someone had hung on it, and beyond it the water changed color, going from pale blue to a darker blue that seemed to have no bottom.

He was looking at the rope when one of the wrestlers broke free and lunged backward and the wave that followed was larger than the others. It hit Marcus at chest height and pushed him off the wall. His feet slipped on the algae. He went sideways, his hand grabbing for the tile and missing, and the slope of the pool floor was under him now, the grade steepening, the water going from waist to chest to chin, and his feet were not on the bottom anymore.

He went under.

The world changed.

Above him the surface was a bright, fractured ceiling, the sun coming through it in shafts and splinters, the light breaking and re-forming, and everything above the water was distorted and far away, as if he were looking up through the wrong end of a telescope. The sound vanished. Not faded — vanished, replaced by the deep underwater quiet, the kind of silence that has weight, that presses against the ears and the temples and the soft places behind the eyes. The chlorine was in his nose and his throat, a chemical burn that tasted like pennies and erasers, and his eyes were open and stinging and the blue was everywhere, above him and below him and around him, and for a moment — one moment, brief and strange — he was not afraid.

The blue was beautiful. The light was beautiful. The silence was beautiful. He was floating in a world that had no noise and no people and no sound of his mother's car grinding its gears and no apartment with thin walls and no books he'd already read and no Saturday mornings alone, and for one moment he thought: *This is what it's like to be nowhere.*

Then his lungs asked for air and nowhere became somewhere and the somewhere was wrong.

He kicked. His arms moved, but they moved the way arms move when they don't know what they're doing, thrashing, pushing water in directions that didn't help. He went up and broke the surface for half a second — long enough to see the sky, white and huge, and to hear the screaming of the other kids, which was the same screaming as before except now it sounded like it was happening on the other side of a door — and then he went under again. Deeper this time. The water was in his mouth. It tasted like the pool and like something else, something sour and organic, and his stomach turned and his chest was burning and his arms were still moving but they were tired now, already tired, and the light above him was getting farther away or he was getting farther from it, which was the same thing.

He was going to die. He understood this the way he understood other things — not with panic, not yet, but with the quiet, resigned comprehension of a boy who read books and knew that the world was full of things that could kill you and that most of them did not care whether you were ready.

Then the panic came. It came all at once, like a hand closing around his chest, and he opened his mouth to scream and the water rushed in and he was choking, drowning, his body convulsing against the thing it could not breathe, and the blue was not beautiful anymore, it was just blue, the color of nothing, the color of the space between the place you were and the place you would never get back to.

A hand.

It came from above, plunging through the bright ceiling of the surface, and it grabbed him — grabbed his arm, just above the elbow, with a grip that was strong and sure and did not hesitate. The hand pulled. Marcus came up through the water like something being born, the surface breaking around him, the air hitting his face, and he gasped and choked and coughed and the sound was ugly, the raw mechanical sound of a body doing the one thing it needed to do.

He was at the edge of the pool. The concrete lip was under his hands. He gripped it and coughed and the water came out of him in long, racking spasms, and someone was next to him, treading water, one hand still on his arm.

"You good, little man?"

Marcus looked up. The boy who had saved him was bigger than him. Twelve, maybe thirteen. Dark-skinned, darker than anyone Marcus knew, with close-cropped hair and a gap between his two front teeth that showed when he grinned, and he was grinning now, a wide, easy grin that had no fear in it, as if pulling drowning kids out of public pools was something he did between breakfast and lunch.

Marcus nodded. He could not speak. The coughing was still in his chest, and his eyes were streaming, and his arms were shaking against the concrete, and the boy's hand was still on his arm, steadying him, and the hand was warm.

"You gotta stay in the shallow end, little man. That deep part ain't no joke."

Marcus nodded again. The boy gave his arm a squeeze — casual, friendly, the way you'd squeeze a teammate's shoulder — and then let go and pushed off the wall and swam away, his strokes long and confident, his body cutting through the water as if the water were glad to get out of his way.

The lifeguard arrived then. A teenager, maybe seventeen, sunburned across the nose, with zinc oxide on his lips and a whistle around his neck that he had clearly not been using. He crouched at the edge of the pool and said, "Hey — hey, you okay? What happened?" and Marcus looked at him and said nothing because there was nothing to say to a person who had not been paying attention when the world almost ended.

Marcus pulled himself out of the pool. He sat on the hot concrete and shook. The sun dried him fast, the water evaporating off his skin and leaving the chlorine behind, a tight, chemical film that made his arms feel like they belonged to someone else. He sat there for a long time. He watched the other kids in the water. He watched the boy who had saved him, who was now on the other side of the pool, sitting on the deck with his feet in the water, talking to a girl. The boy did not look back at Marcus. He had moved on. For him, it was already over.

His mother came at three. She pulled into the lot and walked through the gate and found him sitting on the bench by the changing rooms, dressed, dry, his towel folded on his lap. She looked at his face and her expression changed, the way a sky changes when a cloud moves across it, and she said, "What happened?"

He told her. He told her about the deep end and the water and the hand that grabbed him, and as he told it the words came out flat and precise, the way words come out of a child who is trying very hard to describe something that was larger than his language, and his mother's face went through several things — fear, anger, relief, the particular fury of a parent who has left her child somewhere and the somewhere has betrayed her trust — and then she pulled him against her and held him, her chin on top of his head, and she said what she always said when the world proved dangerous and she had not been there to stand between him and it.

"Your grandmother said angels would protect you."

Marcus did not say anything. He was eight years old and he did not believe in angels. He was eight years old and already skeptical, already the kind of person who wanted evidence, who looked at the world and saw mechanisms and causes and effects and not the invisible hand of anything. The boy who had saved him was not an angel. He was a boy. He was a twelve-year-old with a gap-toothed grin and strong hands who had jumped in the water because Marcus was drowning and someone needed to jump.

But he did not say this to his mother. He let her hold him, and he pressed his face into her shirt, which smelled like Windex and the 7 train and the detergent she bought in bulk from the dollar store, and he thought about the boy's face, which he could already feel starting to fade, the details blurring the way details blur when you try to hold them too tightly, and he decided to hold it anyway. The face. The grin. The hand.

He would hold it for twenty-eight years.

***

The pool was free and it was close and it was better than the apartment, which was a fourth-floor walkup on Fulton where the windows didn't open all the way and the air conditioner had been broken since April and nobody was coming to fix it because the landlord was a man named Greevey who lived in New Jersey and considered his tenants a species of pest that had infested his investment property. David Okonkwo was twelve years old and he understood these things — landlords and broken air conditioners and the particular arithmetic of being poor in a neighborhood that was getting poorer — the way other twelve-year-olds understood baseball stats or video game cheat codes. It was the knowledge of his environment. He wore it without thinking about it.

His cousin Ray had dropped him off at the pool at eleven. Ray was nineteen and already part of things that David understood but did not participate in, things that happened after dark and involved cars that idled at corners and money that appeared and disappeared according to rules that were not written down. Ray was not a bad person. David knew this the way he knew most things about people — by watching. Ray bought David sneakers when David's sneakers wore through. Ray gave David's mother cash when the gas got shut off. Ray did these things without being asked, and he did them with the casual, offhand generosity of someone who had money and no particular reason to keep it, and if the money came from places that would put Ray in prison eventually, well, that was the arithmetic. You did the math with the numbers you had.

"Stay out of trouble," Ray had said through the window of the Civic, his arm draped over the steering wheel, a gold chain catching the sun against his collarbone. His eyes were already somewhere else — his phone, the corner, wherever he went when he left David places. "I'll be back around two."

"Aight."

David walked through the gate. The pool was crowded, the way it always was in July, the deck thick with bodies and towels and coolers and the particular chaos of a neighborhood that had one public amenity and used it accordingly. The concrete was hot enough to hurt through the soles of his flip-flops. He found a spot along the fence, dropped his towel, pulled off his shirt, and sat down.

He did not get in the water right away. David did not do most things right away. He was a watcher. He had been a watcher for as long as he could remember — the kid in the classroom who sat in the back and tracked the room, who knew which kids were about to fight before the kids themselves knew, who could read the teacher's mood from the way she set down her coffee cup. Watching was how David understood the world, the way other people understood it through conversation or books or the steady accumulation of being told. Nobody had told David much. His mother worked. His father was a name on a certificate and a face in a photograph and nothing else. The telling had not happened, so the watching had filled the space.

He watched the pool. He noted the lifeguard — a white kid with a sunburn, sitting in the elevated chair, his whistle hanging unused against his chest. The lifeguard was talking to a girl who stood at the base of the chair with her hip cocked and her hand shading her eyes, and the conversation had the specific cadence of a boy trying to impress a girl, the sentences getting longer, the laughter coming too quickly. David noted this and filed it: lifeguard was not watching the water. The lifeguard was watching the girl. This was a piece of information, and David collected pieces of information the way some people collected coins or stamps, sorting them, holding them, turning them over to see what they were worth.

He watched the kids in the water. The younger ones were in the shallow end, their bodies low, their splashing constrained, orbiting the adults who stood waist-deep with crossed arms and watchful faces. The older ones were in the deep end, doing backflips off the edge, racing each other to the far wall, their confidence loud and physical, their bodies claiming space the way older kids' bodies always did. Between the two groups was the middle water, the transitional zone where the shallow end sloped toward the deep, and this was where the trouble would happen if trouble happened, because the slope was gradual and a kid who wasn't paying attention could drift from safe to dangerous without noticing the grade change under his feet.

David's eyes moved across the pool in a pattern he was not conscious of — left to right, shallow to deep, edge to center. He saw the little kid almost immediately. Small. Asian, maybe. Young — seven, eight. Standing alone near the wall of the pool with his hand trailing against the tile, his body tense the way a body gets when it is trying not to be noticed. The kid was not swimming. He was moving along the edge, staying close to the wall, keeping distance between himself and the roughhousing in the center, and David recognized the posture because he had worn it himself at that age — the posture of someone navigating a space that was not designed for them, trying to get from one side of it to the other without drawing attention.

David watched the kid. He watched him move along the wall toward the corner where the shallow end met the rope line. He watched the older boys wrestling in the center send out waves that rocked the small body against the tile. He watched the kid's face, which was focused and serious and a little bit scared, the face of someone doing math in his head — calculating distances, depths, the probability of being knocked off his feet.

Then the wave came. One of the wrestlers broke free and lunged and the displacement sent a roller across the pool, and the small kid lost his grip on the wall and slipped sideways and his feet went out from under him and he slid down the slope into the deeper water and went under.

David's body was already moving before the thought formed. He did not decide to jump. There was no calculation, no weighing of options, no moment of hesitation in which he considered whether this was his business or somebody else's. The kid went under and David was on his feet and crossing the deck in three strides and then he was in the air and then he was in the water, the warm chlorine closing over him, his eyes open, his arms reaching.

The kid was two feet below the surface. His arms were going in the wrong directions, the uncoordinated flailing of a body that did not know how to swim, and bubbles were coming out of his mouth in a thin, desperate stream. David grabbed him. He got his hand around the kid's upper arm and pulled, kicked hard, and broke the surface with the boy's head against his shoulder.

The kid coughed. A violent, whole-body cough that shook him against David's chest, water pouring out of his mouth, his small hands gripping David's arm with a strength that surprised him. David kicked to the wall and hooked one arm over the concrete lip and held on, the kid still gripping him, still coughing, the sound of it raw and terrible and alive.

"You good, little man?"

The kid looked up at him. His eyes were red from the chlorine and his face was streaked with water and snot and his chest was heaving, and he nodded.

David grinned. He couldn't help it. The grin came from the same place the jump had come from — somewhere below the part of him that calculated and watched and filed information, somewhere in the basement of who he was where things were simple. The kid had been drowning. David had pulled him out. It was the least complicated thing he had done in years.

"You gotta stay in the shallow end, little man. That deep part ain't no joke."

The kid nodded again. David gave his arm a squeeze and let go and pushed off the wall. He swam back to his spot along the fence and pulled himself out and sat down and the whole thing, start to finish, had taken less than forty seconds.

The lifeguard showed up then, climbing down from his chair with the belated urgency of someone who had just realized he was supposed to be doing his job. He crouched by the kid and asked questions, and the kid answered or didn't, and David watched from across the deck and felt something that he could not name. Not pride, exactly. Not satisfaction. Something quieter than either of those, something that sat in his chest like a warm stone. He had done a good thing. A simple, uncomplicated, good thing. He had seen someone in trouble and he had gone to them and he had pulled them out, and there was no angle to it, no arithmetic, no cost-benefit analysis, no favor owed. Just a kid drowning and a bigger kid jumping in.

He felt good. He felt actually, genuinely good, in a way he did not usually feel, because in David's life good feelings came with conditions attached — you felt good because Ray had given you money, but the money was dirty; you felt good because your mother had a day off, but the day off meant less on the check; you felt good because somebody on the block respected you, but the respect was for things you did not want to be respected for. The goodness was always tangled up in something else. But this — this was clean. This was a straight line between seeing and doing, no detours, no complications, and the feeling it produced was like cold water on a hot day, and David sat on the concrete deck in the July sun and let it sit inside him.

Ray pulled up at two. David grabbed his towel and his shirt and walked through the gate and got in the Civic, which smelled like air freshener and something sweeter underneath it, and Ray pulled out of the lot without asking about his day.

David told him anyway. He told him about the kid and the drowning and the grabbing and the pulling, and as he told it the warm stone in his chest got warmer, and he was smiling, the gap in his teeth showing, his hands moving the way his hands moved when he was telling a story he cared about.

Ray listened. He nodded once. Then he said, "Nah, for real?" And then, "That's what I'm talking about." And then, quieter, glancing at David sideways with an expression that was not quite a smile and not quite a frown: "Too soft though. Gonna get you killed."

David stopped smiling.

He looked out the window. The neighborhood slid past — bodegas with their awnings down, a barbershop with men sitting outside in folding chairs, kids on the corner, a fire hydrant open and spraying water into the street in a silver arc that caught the light and threw it back. The Civic's engine rattled. Ray turned up the radio. The warm stone in David's chest was still there, but something had settled over it now, a layer of something colder, and David thought about what Ray had said and knew that Ray was not wrong. In this neighborhood, in this life, the math was different. You did not jump into water for strangers. You watched. You calculated. You kept your head down and your hands clean — or you didn't keep your hands clean, but you kept them to yourself. You survived.

But the stone was still warm. Under the cold layer, it persisted. It would persist through what came next — the crew, the corner, the night that went wrong, the courtroom, the sentence, the fifteen years of cells and counts and the slow erosion of days in a place where the walls were the same color as the pool water, a flat institutional blue that held nothing inside it. Through all of it, the stone persisted. The memory of a day when he saw someone drowning and did not calculate. When he jumped. When the jump was enough.

It was the same instinct. The same place in the basement of who he was, the place where things were simple. It was the instinct that would make him, twenty years later, throw himself at a sixteen-year-old with a gun in a school hallway, a tackle that would earn him three bullets and a punctured lung and a photograph on the front page of a newspaper that called him a hero, a word he never used for himself because heroes made decisions and David had not decided anything. He had seen someone in trouble and he had gone to them. The same straight line. The same clean geometry.

The Civic turned onto Fulton. Ray parked in front of the building. David got out, closed the door, took his towel and his shirt, and walked up four flights of stairs to the apartment where the air conditioner did not work and the windows did not open all the way, and he sat on his bed in the heat and pressed his palms together and looked at his hands — the same hands that had grabbed the boy, the same hands that would, in six years, hold things he should not have held, in a neighborhood that punished softness the way weather punished exposed skin — and he thought about the kid's face. The red eyes. The small hands gripping his arm.

He did not know the kid's name. He would not remember the kid's face. But the feeling — the clean, uncomplicated goodness of having pulled someone out of deep water — would stay. Buried under everything that came after, it would stay. A warm stone in a cold place, evidence that he was capable of something that had nothing to do with the arithmetic of survival.

The angel on the park bench was not supposed to be here.

Cassiel sat on the green wooden slats with her hands in her coat pockets — she wore a coat even in July, because two hundred years of embodiment had not cured her of being cold, a paradox she had stopped trying to explain to herself — and watched the pool through the chain-link fence. She was between assignments. Her last case, a woman in Astoria deciding whether to testify against her landlord, had resolved two days ago, and her next case had not yet come through the system, which meant she was in the gap, the administrative pause between one human's crisis and another's, and in the gap Cassiel did what she always did: she walked. She sat. She watched.

She was not assigned to anyone at this pool. She had no case file open, no margin to work, no whisper to deliver. She was here because the bench was in the shade and the shade was cool and because she had been walking for three hours and her feet hurt, and because the sound of children in water was, for reasons she had never examined, a sound that made the tiredness in her easier to carry. Not lighter. Easier.

She watched the pool the way she watched most things — with the practiced, panoramic attention of someone who had been observing human behavior for two centuries and had learned to see patterns the way a meteorologist sees weather, not as individual events but as systems, pressures, fronts colliding and diverging. She saw the lifeguard's inattention. She saw the social dynamics among the children, the hierarchies of age and size and confidence asserting themselves in the water the same way they asserted themselves on sidewalks and in schoolyards. She saw the small boy moving along the wall, and she noted him the way she noted all solitary children in public spaces, with the low-grade vigilance that was her professional default.

She saw him go under.

Her body tensed. Two hundred years of instinct gathered in her shoulders and her hands, the automatic preparation for intervention, the readiness to stand and move and lean toward the fence and find the frequency, the whisper, the nudge that might reach someone — the lifeguard, perhaps, or one of the adults in the shallow end — and redirect their attention to the child in the water.

But before she could move, the other boy moved. The bigger one, the dark-skinned twelve-year-old who had been sitting along the fence with his towel in his lap, watching the pool with an attentiveness that Cassiel had noticed and admired without knowing why. He was on his feet and crossing the deck and in the air before the small boy's head had been under for three seconds.

Cassiel's hands relaxed in her pockets. She sat back against the bench. She watched the bigger boy pull the smaller one to the surface, watched the coughing and the gripping and the moment at the wall where the twelve-year-old grinned and asked if the kid was good. She watched the lifeguard arrive late. She watched the bigger boy swim back to his spot and sit down, and she watched the look on his face — the quiet, private look of someone who has done something that makes sense to them in a way that most things do not.

No angel had intervened.

The thought settled into her slowly, the way a stone settles through water, and it carried a weight that surprised her. She had been doing this work for two hundred and six years, and in those years she had come to understand her role as necessary — not glamorous, not grand, but necessary in the way that a bridge is necessary, spanning the gap between what humans could do on their own and what they needed help to do. She whispered. They listened, or didn't. She nudged. They moved, or didn't. But the architecture of it assumed the nudge was needed. The architecture assumed that without the whisper, without the presence of an angel leaning into the frequency of a human's better instincts, the instincts would not fire. That the gap was real. That the bridge was doing the work.

But the boy had jumped. No whisper. No nudge. No angel anywhere near the frequency of his decision-making. He had seen a child drowning and he had gone into the water, and the impulse had come from a place that Cassiel had no claim to, a place that preceded her involvement and operated independent of her systems and her protocols and her two centuries of invisible labor.

He had just chosen.

She sat on the bench and felt something shift inside her, a tectonic adjustment, slight but structural. It was not doubt, exactly. It was not the corrosive, accumulating doubt that she carried with her the way she carried the tiredness, the doubt that whispered — in a frequency she could hear perfectly well — that the work was pointless, that the margins were an illusion, that free will did not need an angel's thumb on the scale to tip toward good. It was something less certain than doubt and more dangerous. It was a question.

*If they can do this without us, what are we for?*

She watched the boy's mother arrive. She watched the boy — Marcus, though Cassiel did not know his name and would not learn it for another twenty years — tell his mother what had happened, his small face serious, his words precise. She watched the mother pull him close, and she read the woman's lips through the chain-link: *Your grandmother said angels would protect you.* Cassiel looked away.

She watched the bigger boy leave with his cousin. The cousin's car was a Honda, dark green, with tinted windows and a bass line leaking from the speakers. The boy got in. The car pulled away. Cassiel watched it turn the corner and disappear into the grid of the neighborhood, carrying the boy back into a life that she could read from here — the trajectory was visible in the cousin's posture, in the car, in the gold chain, in the particular way the cousin's eyes moved when he scanned the street, the way a person's eyes move when they are always calculating, always reading the room for threat and opportunity. The boy was twelve. The trajectory would claim him before he was eighteen.

And yet.

The boy had jumped. Without prompting, without intervention, without the machinery of heaven leaning on the fulcrum of his conscience. He had seen a child drowning and his body had moved before his mind could calculate, and in that gap — the gap between seeing and thinking, between instinct and decision — there was something that Cassiel could not account for. Something that did not appear in her reports or her case files or the probability matrices that the system used to identify and prioritize margin cases. Something that was, in the most literal sense, unassigned.

She would not file a report. There was nothing to report. She had not intervened. She had not been assigned. The incident at the Prospect Park Public Pool on a Saturday in July 2004 would not appear in any angel's records, would not be discussed at any support meeting, would not factor into any analysis of margin case outcomes or intervention efficiency. It would exist only in the memories of the people who were there — the boy who almost drowned, who would carry the face of his rescuer for twenty-eight years; the boy who jumped, who would carry the feeling of clean goodness through fifteen years of prison; and an angel on a park bench who had watched a human being do the thing she had spent two centuries helping humans do, and who had not been needed.

Cassiel sat on the bench for a long time after both boys were gone. The pool noise continued, the screaming and splashing, the lifeguard's whistle finally blowing, belatedly, at some other minor infraction. The sun moved. The shade shifted. The bench grew warm beneath her, and the warmth was ordinary, the warmth of wood that had absorbed the heat of a summer day, and there was nothing supernatural about it.

She stood up. Her knees protested. She pulled her coat tighter, a habit so old it was indistinguishable from reflex, and she walked away from the pool, her footsteps quiet on the sidewalk, heading nowhere in particular, because she was between assignments and the gap was the gap and there was nowhere she needed to be.

Behind her, the pool glittered. Twenty children screamed and laughed and splashed in water that did not care whether they lived or drowned. A lifeguard blew his whistle.
